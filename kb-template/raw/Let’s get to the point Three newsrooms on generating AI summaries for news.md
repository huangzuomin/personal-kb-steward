“Summaries aren’t a replacement for journalism: they can’t exist without it.” The Wall Street Journal, Bloomberg, and Yahoo News on what they’ve learned rolling out AI-powered summaries.

Takeaways. Summaries. Key points. Whatever you call them, bullet point lists summarizing articles are popping up on more and more news sites.

The technology powering these summaries is evolving even as newsrooms find themselves pushed to adopt and adapt. The results have not always been perfect or, uh, [bulletproof](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/03/29/business/media/bloomberg-ai-summaries.html). But three news organizations out in front on this technology and reliably generating reader-friendly summaries are The Wall Street Journal, Bloomberg, and Yahoo News.

I had seen some rumors that this kind of “key point” can help surface the articles in Google and other search engines. Wall Street Journal director of newsroom SEO Ed Hyatt said that there was likely some truth to that.

“We don’t have strong evidence to suggest that the Key Points help with search indexing, but certainly it can help,” Hyatt said. “It’s generally optimized text at the top of the story which is great for both readers and Googlebot to get a quick understanding of what the content is about.”

Primarily, though, news organizations that have rolled out AI-powered summaries see the feature as a service for busy readers. The key takeaway? These newsrooms are seeing enough interest to keep experimenting.

The news org and aggregator Yahoo News has developed a “Key Takeaways” feature for some articles on its site. The takeaways are intended to summarize the full article — but also to entice readers to go ahead and read the whole thing.

Unlike other summaries on this list, the takeaways on Yahoo News are opt-in. Interested readers must click “Generate Key Takeaways”:

Here’s one example on an Associated Press piece about a Supreme Court decision [handed down](https://www.yahoo.com/news/supreme-court-makes-easier-claim-140833706.html) on Thursday:

[![](https://www.niemanlab.org/images/Screenshot-2025-06-05-at-12.41.51%E2%80%AFPM.png)](https://www.niemanlab.org/images/Screenshot-2025-06-05-at-12.41.51%E2%80%AFPM.png)

Yahoo News debuted Key Takeaways in 2024 as [it relaunched its app](https://www.yahoo.com/tech/yahoo-news-gets-an-ai-powered-overhaul-171507596.html) to include a whole host of AI-powered features. (One allows users to flag “clickbait” headlines, which then get rewritten by AI.)

[Kat Downs Mulder](https://x.com/katdowns?lang=en), general manager of Yahoo News, said the acquisition of [the app Artifact](https://www.yahooinc.com/press/yahoo-announces-the-acquisition-of-artifact-the-news-discovery-platform-created-by-instagram-cofounders-kevin-systrom-and-mike-krieger) “really accelerated” the newsroom’s AI development process.

“Key takeaways on Yahoo are about making the reading experience easier for our users,” Downs Mulder said. “We think of them as a convenience feature, not a replacement for the full article.”

The summaries were deliberately built to “only pull information from the article itself, rather than bringing in information from across the internet,” Downs Mulder noted. “This significantly reduces the chances of errors or inaccuracies showing up in the summaries.”

Key Takeaways went through several rounds of “extensive testing” before being rolled out. Readers can flag summaries they find unhelpful.

“We have multiple quality checks in our process, including human review,” Downs Mulder said. She said readers typically rate the summaries as accurate. They also seem to be responding well to the relaunched app and its AI-powered features. User engagement increased by 50% and time spent per user rose 165% since the relaunch, she noted.

“We’re careful to ensure that AI enhances rather than detracts from the user experience, and we always incorporate human curation alongside AI,” Downs Mulder said.

Over at The Wall Street Journal, AI-generated summaries come as three bullet points called “Key Points.” Here’s what the feature looks like on [an exclusive](https://www.wsj.com/economy/cpi-inflation-data-accuracy-8bd2a8ae) about economists questioning the U.S. inflation data:

[![](https://www.niemanlab.org/images/Screenshot-2025-06-05-at-2.29.06%E2%80%AFPM.png)](https://www.niemanlab.org/images/Screenshot-2025-06-05-at-2.29.06%E2%80%AFPM.png)

“Trust and transparency with our audiences are core to our mission,” said [Tess Jeffers](https://www.linkedin.com/in/tess-jeffers), director of newsroom data and AI for The Wall Street Journal. Every AI-generated summary prominently features a “What’s this?” button that quickly explains the feature to readers.

“An artificial-intelligence tool created this summary, which was based on the text of the article and checked by an editor,” the Journal tells readers who click on the “What’s this?” button. “ about how we use artificial intelligence in our journalism.”

The Journal first began working on the feature in early 2024.

“Initially, the work was scoped for our Newswires product, targeted towards B2B clients who want key information without necessarily reading the full article text,” Jeffers noted. “Once the AI workflow was built into the CMS, however, we could leverage the summaries in other places.”

Before the summaries went live on the site, the Journal’s newsroom worked with the tech and product teams to craft a prompt that generated high-quality summaries and evaluated the summaries for accuracy.

Then, the key points — which are powered by Google Gemini — were shown to a random set of users via an A/B test. The Journal was laser-focused on two questions: Did the key points impact subscriber engagement with its journalism (volume of articles read per session, length of session, or length of time spent on an article with key points)? And did they impact nonsubscriber conversion rate? Following the tests, the Journal began including the summaries on all core news articles.

The summary tool is built directly into the Journal’s CMS and, once generated, the bullet points follow the same workflow as the story itself with the newsroom reviewing for accuracy, clarity, and house style.

“It’s up to the editor’s discretion to apply or remove the Key Points to their articles,” Jeffers said. “Generally, the Key Points make the most sense on our core news stories where the facts are clear.”

The underlying AI technology is continuously evolving, Jeffers noted, requiring regular updates to the AI model and the Journal’s prompt to generate accurate and helpful summaries.

“One lesson is that this technology requires regular care and maintenance,” she said. “The generative AI technology is constantly changing, which means we also have to be ready for active development. As older LLM models phase out, we have had to update to newer models, then re-test and evaluate to ensure the output still passes QA.”

“Another lesson is that given where the technology is right now, a human-in-the-loop is critical,” she added. “While we’re quite happy that the error rates are very low, they’re not zero. As other publishers have noticed, even that low error rate could lead to a number of correctable errors.”

The Journal plans to experiment with more AI-generated features. It’s still working out the best way to present readers with the option to read AI-generated key points or dive deep, depending on their preference. Jeffers described generative AI as “transformative in helping expand the reach and impact of our [Newswires](https://www.dowjones.com/professional/newswires/) business,” in particular.

“We’ve launched new products in Korean and Japanese and expanded our footprint in other languages, thanks to AI (and a super skilled team working at the cutting edge of genAI language translation),” she said. “We’re also very excited about how generative AI can help us [super serve our audiences](https://www.niemanlab.org/2025/01/audience-editors-offer-advice-for-dispiriting-times-in-social-and-search/) while continuing to deliver exceptional and distinctive journalism. Our chatbots (Lars, the [Taxbot](https://www.wsj.com/personal-finance/taxes/taxes-tax-season-ai-chatbot-lars-ebf9b410?gaa_at=eafs&gaa_n=ASWzDAgFd2ByEKNaFMSDJPMSYq1JnEaUFHQ63VHG5VxDuBI5xMzWG3xRRLgl96cDiAg%3D&gaa_ts=6841fcf3&gaa_sig=xaC0vbXVPzCsOE4_nkNlLP_7TKbaAnCpw9U9wx4lraU7I_eep5ocq4tF3z-U9TdNYbREHykP6Xa4wIOmMJ1xJg%3D%3D) and [Joannabot](https://www.wsj.com/tech/personal-tech/iphone-16-review-ai-joannabot-3d7fce19?gaa_at=eafs&gaa_n=ASWzDAj37hjNCibPZLT_JOJF6kUXcCdt-_B5o7dZmH9QpF_F5R57nNDxmYJ7NO9DcOQ%3D&gaa_ts=6841fcfe&gaa_sig=vf24Ea_7twX8XIZ-qRpmfhqPVjj1VIAFdLwGjlz62HT3KETcZuyMXSK33VApM5DTHfCYR-mNX6JtwMs77_KCeQ%3D%3D)) are fantastic tools to help our readers explore topics where we have deep expertise and authority. And our investigative and data journalism teams are leveraging AI to help power their reporting, enabling analysis of complicated datasets, such as TikTok videos and social media exchanges.”

I’ll confess I find the tariffs story bewildering. Every day it seems there are more pauses and exemptions and [TACO trades](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-05-28/trump-insists-to-wall-street-he-s-no-chicken-on-tariff-policy?sref=1hpvwfy8) to read about. It turns out I’m not the only one. Bloomberg News is finding news stories that generate *a lot* of coverage are a great use-case for AI-generated summaries. They can help readers stay “oriented and informed” amid a deluge of individual news stories, said [Chris Collins](https://www.linkedin.com/in/chriscollinsnyc/), chief product officer of news at Bloomberg.

The AI summaries on Bloomberg.com are called Takeaways. AI summaries also appear on Bloomberg stories on the Bloomberg Terminal. In general, very short stories do not have AI-generated summaries. They do appear, however, on some breaking news articles. Soon after [the Xi-Trump call](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-06-05/trump-xi-speak-amid-trade-technology-dispute-xinhua-says?sref=1hpvwfy8), for example, Bloomberg had published the following:

[![](https://www.niemanlab.org/images/Screenshot-2025-06-05-at-12.40.45%E2%80%AFPM.png)](https://www.niemanlab.org/images/Screenshot-2025-06-05-at-12.40.45%E2%80%AFPM.png) Keep scrolling on the Takeaways and you’ll find a video and related stories that help readers connect the dots on the issues. On [a piece about Trump falling out with Elon Musk](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-06-05/trump-musk-alliance-unravels-in-split-over-big-beautiful-bill?sref=1hpvwfy8), Bloomberg highlighted a video of the president saying he’s “very disappointed” in the billionaire and links to Musk’s most recent criticism.

[![](https://www.niemanlab.org/images/Screenshot-2025-06-05-at-2.06.49%E2%80%AFPM.png)](https://www.niemanlab.org/images/Screenshot-2025-06-05-at-2.06.49%E2%80%AFPM.png)

Bloomberg began testing AI summaries at the end of 2024 and then rolled them out more broadly at the beginning of this year. They’re on a limited but growing number of articles. Bloomberg features them on longform pieces as well and plans to include them on its opinion pieces in the future.

Collins described the summaries as “a clear, quick snapshot.”

“We publish thousands of stories every day,” Collins noted. “Especially at such a busy time for news, readers tell us they want to stay on top of what’s happening and quickly get caught up on important information.”

“The summaries are designed to enhance the reading experience and complement our journalism – they are not a substitute for the depth, context, and analysis that our reporters provide,” he added.

Reader feedback — both in comments to Bloomberg and what the newsroom has seen in the data — has been very positive, Collins said. The summaries are especially welcome on fast-moving news.

“With summaries, we’re trying to help readers stay on top of news. The tariffs coverage is a great use case: it’s an area that has generated a high volume of coverage recently; summaries can help our readers stay oriented and informed,” Collins said.

I asked Collins about any lessons learned from the experiment.

“It’s important to start with a strong understanding of your audience, and their needs from content and the overall experience,” he said. “Look to use AI to improve the user experience, not simply because a new technology is available. Summaries aren’t a replacement for journalism: they can’t exist without it.”

“摘要不能取代新闻业：没有新闻业就没有摘要。”《华尔街日报》、彭博社和雅虎新闻分享了他们在推出AI驱动摘要功能中学到的经验。

要点。摘要。关键信息。无论你如何称呼它们，以项目符号列表形式总结文章的功能正出现在越来越多的新闻网站上。

驱动这些摘要的技术正在不断发展，新闻编辑室也发现自己被推向采用和适应这些技术。结果并非总是完美无缺，或者说，并非总是[滴水不漏](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/03/29/business/media/bloomberg-ai-summaries.html)。但在这项技术上走在前列并能可靠地生成对读者友好的摘要的三家新闻机构是《华尔街日报》、彭博社和雅虎新闻。

我曾听说过一些传言，称这种“关键要点”有助于文章在谷歌和其他搜索引擎中获得更好的展示。 《华尔街日报》新闻编辑室SEO总监 Ed Hyatt 表示，这很可能属实。

Hyatt 说：“我们没有强有力的证据表明‘关键要点’有助于搜索索引，但这肯定会有帮助。”他补充道：“这通常是文章顶部的优化文本，对于读者和 Googlebot 快速了解内容主旨都非常有益。”

然而，主要而言，推出AI驱动摘要功能的新闻机构将此视为一项为忙碌读者提供的服务。关键的收获是什么？这些新闻编辑室看到了足够的兴趣，足以让他们继续进行实验。

新闻机构兼聚合平台雅虎新闻为其网站上的部分文章开发了“关键要点”（Key Takeaways）功能。这些要点旨在总结全文，同时也吸引读者继续阅读完整文章。

与列表中的其他摘要不同，雅虎新闻的要点是选择加入的。感兴趣的读者必须点击“生成关键要点”：

![[https://www.niemanlab.org/images/Screenshot-2025-06-05-at-1.47.23%E2%80%AFPM.png]]

以下是一个关于美联社报道最高法院周四[做出](https://www.yahoo.com/news/supreme-court-makes-easier-claim-140833706.html)的一项裁决的例子：

![[https://www.niemanlab.org/images/Screenshot-2025-06-05-at-12.41.51%E2%80%AFPM.png]]

雅虎新闻于2024年首次推出“关键要点”功能，当时[他们重新推出了其应用程序](https://www.yahoo.com/tech/yahoo-news-gets-an-ai-powered-overhaul-171507596.html)，并包含了一系列AI驱动的功能。（其中一个功能允许用户标记“标题党”标题，然后由AI重写。）

雅虎新闻总经理 [Kat Downs Mulder](https://x.com/katdowns?lang=en) 表示，收购[Artifact 应用](https://www.yahooinc.com/press/yahoo-announces-the-acquisition-of-artifact-the-news-discovery-platform-created-by-instagram-cofounders-kevin-systrom-and-mike-krieger)“极大地加速了”新闻编辑室的AI开发进程。

Downs Mulder 说：“雅虎的‘关键要点’旨在让用户阅读体验更轻松。”她认为：“我们将它们视为一项便利功能，而不是完整文章的替代品。”

Downs Mulder 指出，这些摘要是特意构建的，旨在“只从文章本身提取信息，而不是从互联网上引入信息”。她表示：“这显著降低了摘要中出现错误或不准确信息的可能性。”

“关键要点”在推出前经过了多轮“广泛测试”。读者可以标记他们认为无用的摘要。

Downs Mulder 说：“我们的流程中有多个质量检查环节，包括人工审核。”她表示，读者通常认为摘要是准确的。他们似乎也对重新推出的应用程序及其AI驱动功能反应良好。她指出，自重新推出以来，用户参与度提高了50%，每位用户花费的时间增加了165%。

Downs Mulder 说：“我们谨慎确保AI是增强而非削弱用户体验，并且我们始终将人工编辑与AI结合使用。”

在《华尔街日报》，AI生成的摘要以三个项目符号点的形式呈现，称为“关键要点”（Key Points）。以下是该功能在关于经济学家质疑美国通胀数据的[一篇独家报道](https://www.wsj.com/economy/cpi-inflation-data-accuracy-8bd2a8ae)上的样子：

![[https://www.niemanlab.org/images/Screenshot-2025-06-05-at-2.29.06%E2%20PM.png]]

《华尔街日报》新闻编辑室数据与AI总监 [Tess Jeffers](https://www.linkedin.com/in/tess-jeffers) 表示：“与我们的受众建立信任和透明度是我们使命的核心。”每个AI生成的摘要都显著地带有一个“这是什么？”按钮，可以快速向读者解释该功能。

点击“这是什么？”按钮的读者会看到《华尔街日报》的解释：“此摘要由人工智能工具生成，基于文章文本，并经过编辑审核。了解我们如何在新闻业中使用人工智能。”

《华尔街日报》于2024年初开始开发此功能。

Jeffers 指出：“最初，这项工作是针对我们的 Newswires 产品进行的，面向希望快速获取关键信息而无需阅读全文的B2B客户。”她补充说：“然而，一旦AI工作流程被内置到内容管理系统中，我们就可以在其他地方利用这些摘要。”

在摘要上线之前，《华尔街日报》的新闻编辑室与技术和产品团队合作，精心设计了一个能够生成高质量摘要的提示词，并评估了摘要的准确性。

然后，这些由 Google Gemini 提供支持的“关键要点”通过A/B测试展示给随机用户。 《华尔街日报》重点关注两个问题：这些关键要点是否影响了订阅用户对其新闻内容的参与度（每次会话阅读的文章数量、会话时长或在带有关键要点的文章上花费的时间）？它们是否影响了非订阅用户的转化率？测试结束后，《华尔街日报》开始在所有核心新闻文章中包含这些摘要。

摘要工具直接内置在《华尔街日报》的内容管理系统中，生成后，这些项目符号点遵循与文章本身相同的工作流程，由新闻编辑室审核其准确性、清晰度和内部风格。

Jeffers 说：“是否在文章中应用或移除‘关键要点’取决于编辑的判断。”她认为：“通常，‘关键要点’在事实清晰的核心新闻报道中最有意义。”

Jeffers 指出，底层的AI技术在不断发展，需要定期更新AI模型和《华尔街日报》的提示词，以生成准确且有用的摘要。

她说：“一个教训是，这项技术需要定期的维护和保养。”她表示：“生成式AI技术不断变化，这意味着我们也必须做好积极开发的准备。随着旧的LLM模型逐步淘汰，我们不得不更新到新的模型，然后重新测试和评估，以确保输出仍然通过质量保证。”

她补充说：“另一个教训是，考虑到目前的技术水平，人工参与至关重要。”她表示：“虽然我们对错误率非常低感到满意，但错误率并非为零。正如其他出版商所注意到的，即使是这么低的错误率也可能导致一些需要纠正的错误。”

《华尔街日报》计划尝试更多AI生成的功能。他们仍在研究如何最好地向读者提供选择，让他们根据自己的偏好阅读AI生成的关键要点或深入阅读全文。 Jeffers 特别将生成式AI描述为“在帮助扩展我们 [Newswires](https://www.dowjones.com/professional/newswires/) 业务的覆盖范围和影响力方面具有变革性”。

她说：“得益于AI（以及一支在生成式AI语言翻译前沿工作的超级熟练团队），我们推出了韩语和日语的新产品，并扩大了我们在其他语言领域的足迹。”她还表示：“我们也非常高兴看到生成式AI如何帮助我们 [更好地服务我们的受众](https://www.niemanlab.org/2025/01/audience-editors-offer-advice-for-dispiriting-times-in-social-and-search/)，同时继续提供卓越和独特的新闻报道。我们的聊天机器人（Lars，[税务机器人](https://www.wsj.com/personal-finance/taxes/taxes-tax-season-ai-chatbot-lars-ebf9b410?gaa_at=eafs&gaa_n=ASWzDAgFd2ByEKNaFMSDJPMSYq1JnEaUFHQ63VHG5VxDuBI5xMzWG3xRRLgl96cDiAg%3D&gaa_ts=6841fcf3&gaa_sig=xaC0vbXVPzCsOE4_nkNlLP_7TKbaAnCpw9U9wx4lraU7I_eep5ocq4tF3z-U9TdNYbREHykP6Xa4wIOmMJ1xJg%3D%3D) 和 [Joannabot](https://www.wsj.com/tech/personal-tech/iphone-16-review-ai-joannabot-3d7fce19?gaa_at=eafs&gaa_n=ASWzDAj37hjNCibPZLT_JOJF6kUXcCdt-_B5o7dZmH9QpF_F5R57nNDxmYJ7NO9DcOQ%3D&gaa_ts=6841fcfe&gaa_sig=vf24Ea_7twX8XIZ-qRpmfhqPVjj1VIAFdLwGjlz62HT3KETcZuyMXSK33VApM5DTHfCYR-mNX6JtwMs77_KCeQ%3D%3D)）是出色的工具，可以帮助读者探索我们在其中拥有深厚专业知识和权威性的主题。我们的调查和数据新闻团队也正在利用AI来支持他们的报道，从而能够分析复杂的数据集，例如TikTok视频和社交媒体交流。”

我承认我发现关税报道令人困惑。似乎每天都有更多的暂停、豁免和 [TACO 交易](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-05-28/trump-insists-to-wall-street-he-s-no-chicken-on-tariff-policy?sref=1hpvwfy8)需要阅读。事实证明，我不是唯一一个。彭博新闻发现，那些产生大量报道的新闻故事是AI生成摘要的绝佳用例。彭博社新闻首席产品官 [Chris Collins](https://www.linkedin.com/in/chriscollinsnyc/) 表示，它们可以帮助读者在海量新闻报道中保持“方向感和信息通畅”。

Bloomberg.com 上的AI摘要被称为“要点”（Takeaways）。AI摘要也出现在彭博终端上的彭博报道中。一般来说，非常短的文章没有AI生成的摘要。然而，它们确实出现在一些突发新闻文章中。例如，在[习特朗通话](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-06-05/trump-xi-speak-amid-trade-technology-dispute-xinhua-says?sref=1hpvwfy8)后不久，彭博社发布了以下内容：

彭博社于2024年底开始测试AI摘要，并于今年初更广泛地推出。它们出现在数量有限但不断增长的文章中。彭博社也在长篇报道中使用了它们，并计划将来将其包含在观点文章中。

Collins 将这些摘要描述为“清晰、快速的快照”。

Collins 指出：“我们每天发布数千篇报道。”他表示：“尤其是在新闻如此繁忙的时期，读者告诉我们，他们希望及时了解正在发生的事情，并快速掌握重要信息。”

他补充说：“这些摘要旨在增强阅读体验并补充我们的新闻报道——它们不能替代我们记者提供的深度、背景和分析。”

Collins 表示，读者的反馈——无论是直接给彭博社的评论还是新闻编辑室在数据中看到的——都非常积极。这些摘要在快速变化的新闻中尤其受欢迎。

Collins 说：“通过摘要，我们试图帮助读者及时了解新闻。关税报道就是一个很好的用例：这是最近产生大量报道的领域；摘要可以帮助我们的读者保持方向感和信息通畅。”

我问 Collins 关于实验中学到的任何教训。

他说：“重要的是要从对你的受众及其对内容和整体体验的需求有深入了解开始。”他表示：“要利用AI来改善用户体验，而不仅仅是因为有了新技术就使用。摘要不能取代新闻业：没有新闻业就没有摘要。”
