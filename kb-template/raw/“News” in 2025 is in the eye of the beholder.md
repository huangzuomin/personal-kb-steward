人们对内容的分类越来越倾向于“类似新闻”，并且这种分类因平台、来源以及个人而异。

我们有好消息要告诉你！

好吧，*我们*认为是新闻。你可能不同意。也许你觉得它没意思，或者与你的生活无关。也许你以前听说过。（毕竟，[[Nieman Lab]] 之前已经[讨论过这个话题](https://www.niemanlab.org/2023/09/what-is-news-anyway-readers-answers-depend-on-how-much-they-see-people-like-themselves-in-the-story/)。）你甚至可能认为它根本不是新闻。

关键是，根据皮尤研究中心（Pew Research Center）的[一项新研究](https://www.pewresearch.org/journalism/2025/05/13/what-is-news/)，定义新闻已经成为一种个人化和*个性化*的体验，因为做出这些决定的权力已经很大程度上从媒体把关人转移到了普通大众手中。这项研究来自皮尤-奈特倡议（Pew-Knight Initiative），它借鉴了对美国成年人的定性和调查研究，以及对记者和编辑的深入访谈。

我们的定性研究（2024年8月对57位美国人进行）和2025年3月对9482位美国成年人的调查证实了这样一种观点，即新闻的定义因人而异。每个人都根据各种因素——包括他们自己的身份和兴趣——来决定新闻对他们的意义以及他们转向哪些来源。

这些发现建立在我们自己和他人对新闻消费不断变化的动态进行数十年研究的基础上，阐明了新闻过去和现在的关键区别。

在数字和社交媒体兴起之前，研究人员长期以来都是从记者的角度来探讨什么是新闻。新闻的理念通常与[新闻业的机构](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/1461670X.2021.1917445)联系在一起，媒体“把关人”决定什么是有新闻价值的，以特定的语气或[一套价值观](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/14616700118449)制作和包装信息，供被动的受众接收。

“我们正在经历的旅程是，30年前，除了朋友亲口告诉你之外，你可以被告知一些你不知道的事情的平台或场所非常有限，”Axios 的出版人 Nicholas Johnston 说。“现在，它基本上是无限的。”

在数字时代，当人们接触到比以往任何时候都更多的来自更多来源的信息时，包括皮尤研究中心在内的研究人员越来越多地从*受众的角度*研究新闻，因为受众自己定义什么是“新闻”。

“这使得每个人都像一个通讯社编辑，”ProPublica 的执行编辑 Tracy Weber 说。“这很好。但也很糟糕，因为他们没有受过训练成为最好的通讯社编辑。”

受众究竟如何做出这些评估的现实是复杂的。对于大多数人在[数字平台](https://reutersinstitute.politics.ox.ac.uk/trust-gap-how-and-why-news-digital-platforms-viewed-more-sceptically-versus-news-general)和[社交媒体](https://www.pewresearch.org/journalism/2024/06/12/how-americans-get-news-on-tiktok-x-facebook-and-instagram/)上的体验来说，新闻的重要性不如[记者们通常希望的那样](https://www.prospectmagazine.co.uk/ideas/media/69427/irrelevant-unloved-press-lost-touch)。一些研究——包括我们自己的研究——发现人们对新闻的定义与他们的实际行为[并不总是一致](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/21670811.2021.1885986)。人们认为*其他人*认为什么是新闻与[他们自己“感觉”什么是新闻](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/14648849231212737)之间也存在冲突。

![Picture1-2.png](https://www.niemanlab.org/images/Picture1-2.png) 我们的研究发现，如果某件事是真实的、最新的、对社会重要的且公正的，人们通常会认为它更可能是新闻。但超过一半的美国人也表示，他们的消息来源具有与自己相似的政治观点至少在某种程度上是重要的。

对于某件事是“新闻”还是“非新闻”的看法也不是非黑即白的。研究发现，[人们将“新闻”](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/1077699020916808)更多地看作是一个连续体，而不是一个简单的“是”或“否”问题。人们将内容分类为或多或少“类似新闻”，并且这种分类因平台、来源以及个人而异。

![Picture2-3.png](https://www.niemanlab.org/images/Picture2-3.png) 平台和来源：[数字和社交媒体平台](https://www.pewresearch.org/journalism/fact-sheet/news-platform-fact-sheet/)的兴起改变了[人们体验新闻的方式](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/14648849231212737)。人们*在哪里*遇到信息会影响他们体验到的新闻，并且他们的标准通常因[他们发现新闻的方式和地点](https://knightfoundation.org/reports/indicators-of-news-media-trust/)而异。

例如，当我们的定性研究参与者滚动浏览他们自己的社交媒体 feed 时，他们会从与主题及其来源（包括该来源的政治立场）相关的线索中得出结论，从而对信息是否为新闻进行逐个评估。

受众可以并且经常对他们在网上看到的内容类型进行区分，这反映了新闻传播方式不断变化的本质。滚动浏览体验的一部分是对内容是否为新闻以及是否要参与其中做出快速判断。

个人：每个人都带着自己的心态和方法来浏览当今的信息环境。例如，我们的许多参与者承认，他们的个人身份——包括他们的年龄、种族、民族、性别、宗教，*尤其是*他们的政治倾向——会影响他们消费和思考新闻的方式。

它也会影响他们对新闻的感受。人们与他们获得的新闻联系在一起的许多最常见的情绪是负面的：愤怒、悲伤、恐惧、困惑。

![Picture3-1.png](https://www.niemanlab.org/images/Picture3-1.png) 这些感受可能因人们的政治观点和政治背景而异。我们 2025 年 3 月的调查发现，民主党人比共和党人更可能表示他们获得的新闻让他们感到愤怒、悲伤和恐惧。

但绝大多数美国人也表示，新闻至少有时会让他们感到知情。尽管当前的新闻环境存在各种复杂性和挑战，但我们的许多参与者也认为新闻是他们生活中必不可少的一部分。

“我需要知道世界上发生了什么，无论多么令人沮丧，”一位 70 多岁的参与者说。

Do we have news for you!

Well, *we* think it’s news. You might not agree. Maybe you won’t find it interesting, or relevant to your life. Perhaps you’ve heard it before. (After all, Nieman Lab has [addressed this topic](https://www.niemanlab.org/2023/09/what-is-news-anyway-readers-answers-depend-on-how-much-they-see-people-like-themselves-in-the-story/) before.) You might even think it’s not news at all.

The point is, defining news has become a personal and *personalized* experience, as the power to make these decisions has largely shifted from media gatekeepers to the general public, according to a [new Pew Research Center study](https://www.pewresearch.org/journalism/2025/05/13/what-is-news/) from the Pew-Knight Initiative. The study draws on qualitative and survey research with U.S. adults and in-depth interviews with journalists and editors.

Our qualitative research (conducted with 57 Americans in August 2024) and a survey of 9,482 U.S. adults in March 2025 confirm the idea that what news is varies greatly from person to person. And each decides what news means to them and which sources they turn to based on a variety of factors — including their own identities and interests.

These findings build on decades of our own and others’ research on the changing dynamics of news consumption, illuminating key distinctions between what news was and what it is today.

Before the rise of digital and social media, researchers had long approached the question of what news is from the journalist perspective. Ideas of news were generally [tied to the institution of journalism](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/1461670X.2021.1917445) as media “gatekeepers” determined what was newsworthy, producing and packaging information with a particular tone or [set of values](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/14616700118449) for a passive audience.

“The journey we’re on is 30 years ago, the platforms or places where you could be told something you don’t know aside from being personally told by a friend of yours was very limited,” said Nicholas Johnston, the publisher of Axios. “Now, it’s essentially infinite.”

In the digital age, when people are exposed to more information from more sources than ever, researchers — including at Pew Research Center — increasingly study news from the *audience perspective,* as audiences themselves define what “news” is.

“That makes everyone like a wire service editor,” said ProPublica managing editor Tracy Weber. “That’s good. That’s also bad because they’re not trained to be the best wire service editor.”

The reality of exactly how audiences make these assessments is complicated. News is less central to most people’s experiences on [digital platforms](https://reutersinstitute.politics.ox.ac.uk/trust-gap-how-and-why-news-digital-platforms-viewed-more-sceptically-versus-news-general) and [social media](https://www.pewresearch.org/journalism/2024/06/12/how-americans-get-news-on-tiktok-x-facebook-and-instagram/) than [journalists often hope](https://www.prospectmagazine.co.uk/ideas/media/69427/irrelevant-unloved-press-lost-touch). Some work — including our own — finds the definitions people hold for news are [not always consistent](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/21670811.2021.1885986) with their actual behaviors. There are also clashes between what people believe *others* think of as news and [what “feels” like news to them](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/14648849231212737).

![](https://www.niemanlab.org/images/Picture1-2.png) Our study finds that people generally say something is more likely to be news if it is factual, up to date, important to society and unbiased. But more than half of Americans also say it’s at least somewhat important for their news sources to have political views similar to their own.

Opinions on whether something is “news” or “not news” also aren’t black and white. Research has found that [people view “news”](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/1077699020916808) as more of a continuum than a simple yes-or-no question. People classify content as more or less “news-like,” and this varies across platforms and sources, as well as from one person to the next.

![](https://www.niemanlab.org/images/Picture2-3.png) Platforms and sources: The rise of [digital and social media platforms](https://www.pewresearch.org/journalism/fact-sheet/news-platform-fact-sheet/) has changed [how people experience news](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/14648849231212737). [*Where* people encounter information](https://www.taylorfrancis.com/chapters/edit/10.4324/9781003257998-6/news-emily-vraga-stephanie-edgerly) can influence what they experience as news, and their standards often vary depending on [how and where they find it](https://knightfoundation.org/reports/indicators-of-news-media-trust/).

For instance, when our qualitative research participants scrolled through their own social media feeds, they drew from cues related to both the topic and its source — including that source’s political orientation — to make case-by-case evaluations about whether the information was news or not.

It is a reflection of the changing nature of how news is distributed that audiences can, and regularly do, make distinctions about what kinds of content they see online. Part of the scrolling experience is making snap judgments about whether content is news and whether to engage with it.

Individuals: Each person brings their own mindset and approach to navigating today’s information environment. For instance, many of our participants acknowledged that their personal identities — including their age, race, ethnicity, gender, religion and *especially* their political leaning — influence how they consume and think about news.

It also influences how they feel about it. Many of the most common emotions people associate with the news they get are negative: angry, sad, scared, confused.

![](https://www.niemanlab.org/images/Picture3-1.png) These feelings can vary depending on people’s political opinions and the political context. Our March 2025 survey found that Democrats are more likely than Republicans to say the news they get makes them feel angry, sad and scared.

But the vast majority of Americans also say news makes them feel informed at least sometimes. And despite all the complications and challenges of the current news environment, many of our participants also view news as an essential part of their life.

“I need to know what is going on in the world, no matter how upsetting,” said one participant in his 70s.
