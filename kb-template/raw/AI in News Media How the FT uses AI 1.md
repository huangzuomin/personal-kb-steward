| \| # Interview with Liz Lohn at the Financial Times \| \| --- \| \|  \| |     |     |     |     |     |     |
| ----------------------------------------------------------------------- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |

| \|  \| \| --- \| \| **How did you get into your work with AI?**   I’ve been in product for seventeen years now and it’s kind of agnostic of technology. The way you do product work doesn’t really change. You move between scopes and industries and then use whatever technology is best suited to solve the problem.  I worked quite a bit in financial technology where AI was already being used a lot, even before the most recent hype cycle. I was at an open banking startup then built a couple of businesses that leveraged AI for career coaching and property search. I ended up at the Financial Times where a team was created and funded to focus specifically on AI opportunities and challenges in early 2024. I was asked to lead it and that’s where the intersection of AI and newsrooms happened for me       **How is your newsroom currently using AI?**   We use AI across several parts of the workflow. We like to break it down into story finding and story presentation.  Story finding is about helping ingest and make sense of data that’s convoluted, obscure, unstructured or just vast in volume and then generating insights from it.  Story presentation is about supporting the writing, revision and quality control of what gets published. These are tools that support original content creation. It also includes secondary content types like summarising existing articles for readers or smartly searching the archive to surface relevant segments.  We also look at personalisation in how stories are recommended to users, what they read next or listen to next.    **How are your tools available to journalists in the newsroom — can anyone analyse big data sets?**   We try not to monopolise AI as a technology, even though my title includes AI.  There’s a team called Story Innovation, which used to be part of the Accelerate AI team and is now separate. That team looks after investigative tools, building in-house systems like data pipelines for trackers - election trackers, for example - or anything that uses live data surfaced in engaging ways for readers. These tools are closely tied to storytelling.  There’s also FT Professional which serves professional readers with specific needs around combining content with market data and other sources. This involves layers of data ingestion and analysis.  The journalists themselves - especially those in investigative journalism - are often quite tech-savvy. If it’s a one-off story, they’ll build ad hoc solutions. But if the use case is repeatable or scalable, it becomes a capability built by the tech team.  And of course, we’re experimenting with third-party tools. We always check what’s already out there to avoid reinventing the wheel.    **How do you measure the success and impact of AI initiatives?**   It’s easiest on the reader-facing side where we can measure engagement, satisfaction, session depth and time. We’ve got experiments running with bullet-point summaries and other types of content transformation like Ask FT, our Q&A tool. These are easy to track because they link to loyalty, subscriptions and revenue.  On the workflow side, we’re less prescriptive. We avoid tracking task times because that’s not how we view journalism. Instead, we ask whether journalists can do mundane tasks faster now. Surveys help but they’re tricky as small, anonymous samples make it hard to draw strong conclusions.  We also want to know if the tools make certain kinds of reporting more accessible. For example, can people without coding or data skills now do investigative projects they couldn’t before?  And we look at whether we’re enabling stories that wouldn’t have been commissioned in the past because the effort didn’t match expected output. So it’s about improving efficiency, reducing repetitive tasks and making sure we’re not creating more work just to manage the tech.    **Have there been any surprising results or responses to audience- or newsroom-facing tools?**   We launched an experiment with bullet-point summaries using a human-in-the-loop approach. Editors reviewed summaries drafted by AI. One surprise was how little they edited the summaries which for some was almost nothing.    **What advice would you give to newsrooms just starting to explore audience-facing AI tools?**   What we did at first was random and ad hoc. We tried newsletter summaries but they didn’t work because the cross-functional team wrongly assumed what the newsroom needed on its behalf.  We learned that the people writing the news need to decide what’s helpful. So we built a space for them to experiment in the form of an AI playground connected to the LLM where they could explore prompts and test ideas.  So putting tools in users’ hands early is the first piece of advice. It’s also how we developed our reader-facing tools. Editors could test outputs and say what worked and what didn’t. Those learnings shaped what we built.  Another key consideration is scalability. As we create new reader-facing features, we keep asking how to avoid adding extra work for the newsroom.    **How do you stay ahead and integrate new technologies?**   Our data team is excellent and they track new developments constantly.  We keep an eye on what other media outlets are doing and how they’re applying new tech. We’ve got a ChatGPT Deep Research prompt that runs this research monthly for us.  FT Strategies helps too. They’re deeply involved in the industry, do a lot of research and regularly send us summaries.  I also had a mentor last year with a data science background. It was incredibly helpful to talk through problems and get a second opinion from someone outside the FT bubble.      **Are there any AI projects you're working on now that we can expect to see soon?**   This year we’re focusing on content transformation – reader-driven content summaries, article translations into other languages, and text-to-audio conversion.  We’re also experimenting with using a discussion question to drive readers’ attention to the comment section. It is drafted by AI with subsequent human review.    **Are there any training resources you recommend for journalists and editors to get comfortable with AI tools?**   We created an internal task force called AI Fluency. It started with a survey to find out how comfortable people were with AI and what they already knew. Based on that they developed practical training.  The training focused on real newsroom use cases. We also made sure it was a safe space as journalists often deal with sensitive or confidential information so building trust was key. \| |     |     |     |     |     |     |
