“**If I use an AI tool for research or to help me create something, should I cite it in my completed work as a source? How do you properly give attribution to AI tools when you use them?”**

**—Citation Seeker**

Dear Citation,

The straightforward answer is that if you’re using [generative AI](https://www.wired.com/tag/artificial-intelligence/) for research purposes, disclosure is probably not necessary. Yet, attribution is probably required if you use [ChatGPT](https://www.wired.com/tag/chatgpt/) or another AI tool for composition.

Anytime you’re feeling ethically conflicted about disclosing your engagement with AI [software](https://www.wired.com/tag/software/), here are two guiding questions I think you should ask yourself: Did I utilize AI for research or composition? And might the recipient of this [AI-assisted composition](https://www.wired.com/story/how-to-use-chatgpt-canvas-productivity/) feel misled if the tools were revealed to be synthetic instead of organic? Sure, these questions may not map perfectly to every situation, and academics are definitely held to a higher standard when it comes to proper citation, nevertheless I fully believe taking five minutes to reflect can help you understand appropriate usage and avoid unnecessary headaches.

Distinguishing between research and composition is a crucial first step. If I’m using generative AI as a kind of [unreliable encyclopedia](https://www.wired.com/story/google-ai-overviews-broken-how-ai-works/) that can point me toward other sources or broaden my perspective on a topic, but not as part of the actual writing, I think that’s less problematic and unlikely to leave the stench of deception. Always double-check any facts you run across in the chatbot’s outputs, and never reference a ChatGPT output or [Perplexity page](https://www.wired.com/story/perplexity-is-a-bullshit-machine/) as a primary source of truth. Most chatbots can now link to outside sources on the web, so you can click through to read more. Think of it, in this context, as part of the information infrastructure. ChatGPT can be the road you drive on, but the final destination should be some external link.

Let’s say you decide to use a chatbot to sketch out a first draft, or have it come up with writing/images/audio/video to blend with yours. In this case, I think erring on the side of disclosure is smart. Even the Dominos cheese sticks in the Uber Eats app now include a disclaimer that the food description was generated by AI and may list inaccurate ingredients.

Every time you use AI for creation, and in some cases for research, you should be honing in on the second question. Essentially, ask yourself if the reader or viewer would feel tricked by learning later on that portions of what they experienced were generated by AI. If so, you totally should use proper attribution by explaining how you used the tool, out of respect for your audience. Not only would generating parts of this column without disclosure go against [WIRED’s policy](https://www.wired.com/about/generative-ai-policy/), it would also just be a dry and unfun experience for the both of us.

By considering the people who are going to be enjoying your work and your intentions for creating it in the first place, you can add context to your AI usage. That context is helpful for getting through tricky situations. In most cases, a [work email](https://www.wired.com/story/how-to-use-ai-for-email-artificial-intelligence-tips-advice/) generated by AI and proofread by you is probably just fine. Even so, using generative AI to draft a condolence email after a death would be an example of insensitivity—and something that has [actually happened](https://www.cnn.com/2023/02/22/tech/vanderbilt-chatgpt-shooting-email/index.html). If a human on the other side of the communication is seeking to connect with you on a personal, emotional level, consider closing out of that ChatGPT browser tab and pulling out a [notepad and pen](https://www.wired.com/gallery/best-smart-notebooks-and-smart-pens/).

**“How can educators teach adolescents how to use AI tools responsibly and ethically? Do the advantages of AI outweigh the threats?”**

**—Raised Hand**

Dear Raised,

When it comes to education about generative AI, I think we should start young and stay realistic. Kids are beginning to learn computer literacy skills in elementary school and continuing through their senior year of high school. Lessons about safe, effective use of [AI tools](https://www.wired.com/story/6-ai-based-productivity-tools-tested/) would not only help build strong technical skills, but also potentially help students forge a healthy amount of emotional distance from chatbots.

Teachers and parents are rightly worried about kids using generative AI to fraudulently write their essays for them, or using ChatGPT and other homework helpers like [ByteDance’s Gauth AI](https://www.wired.com/story/gauth-ai-math-homework-app/) to quickly grab answers. Lesson plans more focused on in-class practice and discussion may help alleviate this issue. But focusing just on homework misses another looming threat to students. Over the next few years, I expect teenagers to dive further into long, heartfelt, and sometimes inappropriate conversations, not with random strangers online but with sweet-talking chatbots like [Character.AI](https://character.ai/) or [Replika](https://replika.com/).

During a difficult, awkward stage of life, already complicated by the harsh spotlight of contemporary social media, teenagers will likely turn even more inward and asocial, relying on synthetic companions to understand the world around them. Earlier in 2024, a teenager in Florida was an avid user of roleplaying chatbots and confided thoughts of self-harm to the AI before his suicide, [according to reporting from The New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/10/23/technology/characterai-lawsuit-teen-suicide.html). Teaching kids how to safely use AI is not only about avoiding [false information](https://www.wired.com/story/generative-ai-detection-gap/), but also about avoiding unreal relationships and staying tethered to reality.

Whether the advantages of generative AI [in the classroom](https://www.wired.com/story/what-aspects-of-teaching-should-remain-human/) outweigh the threats is a bit of a moot point heading into 2025. The tools have already seeped into the daily lives of students. As educators, equipping these kids with the knowledge and skills to navigate the world around them is paramount. An unquestioning embrace of generative AI may not be smart, but a blind avoidance could be just as catastrophic.

**“如果我将 AI 工具用于研究或帮助我创造一些东西，我是否应该在完成的作品中将其作为来源引用？当你使用 AI 工具时，你如何正确地归功于 AI 工具？”**

**——引用寻求者**

亲爱的引用，

直接的回答是，如果你出于研究目的而使用 [生成式 AI](https://www.wired.com/tag/artificial-intelligence/)，则可能不需要披露。然而，如果你使用 [ChatGPT](https://www.wired.com/tag/chatgpt/) 或其他 AI 工具进行创作，则可能需要归因。

### Gear 通讯：评论、指南和优惠

通过我们的购买指南、优惠和操作指南来提升你的生活，所有指南均经过专家测试。

任何时候，当你对披露你与 AI [软件](https://www.wired.com/tag/software/) 的接触感到道德冲突时，我认为你应该问自己两个指导性问题：我是否将 AI 用于研究或创作？如果向该 [AI 辅助创作](https://www.wired.com/story/how-to-use-chatgpt-canvas-productivity/) 的接收者透露这些工具是合成的而不是有机的，他们是否会感到被误导？当然，这些问题可能不会完美地适用于每种情况，而且在适当引用方面，学术界肯定会被要求达到更高的标准，但我完全相信花五分钟时间进行反思可以帮助你理解适当的使用方式并避免不必要的麻烦。

区分研究和创作是至关重要的一步。如果我将生成式 AI 用作一种 [不可靠的百科全书](https://www.wired.com/story/google-ai-overviews-broken-how-ai-works/)，它可以让我了解其他来源或拓宽我对某个主题的视角，但不是作为实际写作的一部分，我认为这不太成问题，也不太可能留下欺骗的恶臭。务必仔细检查你在聊天机器人的输出中遇到的任何事实，切勿将 ChatGPT 输出或 [Perplexity 页面](https://www.wired.com/story/perplexity-is-a-bullshit-machine/) 作为主要的真实来源。现在大多数聊天机器人都可以链接到网络上的外部来源，因此你可以单击以阅读更多内容。在此背景下，将它视为信息基础设施的一部分。ChatGPT 可以是你驾驶的道路，但最终目的地应该是某个外部链接。

假设你决定使用聊天机器人来勾勒初稿，或者让它想出与你的内容相融合的文字/图像/音频/视频。在这种情况下，我认为谨慎披露是明智的。即使是 Uber Eats 应用中的 Dominos 芝士棒现在也包含一项免责声明，指出食物描述是由 AI 生成的，并且可能列出不准确的成分。

每次你将 AI 用于创作，在某些情况下用于研究时，你都应该关注第二个问题。从本质上讲，问问你自己，如果读者或观众后来得知他们经历的部分内容是由 AI 生成的，他们是否会感到受骗。如果是这样，你完全应该通过解释你是如何使用该工具的来正确归因，以尊重你的受众。在不披露的情况下生成本专栏的部分内容不仅违反了 [WIRED 的政策](https://www.wired.com/about/generative-ai-policy/)，而且对我们俩来说也只是一次枯燥无趣的经历。

通过考虑那些会欣赏你的作品和最初创作它的意图的人，你可以为你的 AI 使用情况添加背景。该背景有助于解决棘手的情况。在大多数情况下，由 AI 生成的 [工作电子邮件](https://www.wired.com/story/how-to-use-ai-for-email-artificial-intelligence-tips-advice/) 并由你校对可能很好。即便如此，在死亡后使用生成式 AI 起草慰问电子邮件将是麻木不仁的一个例子——而且是 [实际发生的事情](https://www.cnn.com/2023/02/22/tech/vanderbilt-chatgpt-shooting-email/index.html)。如果沟通的另一方寻求在个人、情感层面上与你建立联系，请考虑关闭该 ChatGPT 浏览器标签并拿出 [记事本和笔](https://www.wired.com/gallery/best-smart-notebooks-and-smart-pens/)。

**“教育工作者如何教导青少年负责任和道德地使用 AI 工具？AI 的优势是否超过了威胁？”**

**——举手发言**

亲爱的举手，

在对生成式 AI 进行教育时，我认为我们应该从小开始，保持现实。孩子们开始在小学学习计算机素养技能，并一直持续到高中的最后一年。关于 [AI 工具](https://www.wired.com/story/6-ai-based-productivity-tools-tested/) 的安全有效使用的课程不仅有助于培养强大的技术技能，而且还有可能帮助学生与聊天机器人建立一定程度的情感距离。

老师和家长们理所当然地担心孩子们使用生成式 AI 为他们欺诈性地写论文，或者使用 ChatGPT 和其他家庭作业助手，如 [字节跳动的 Gauth AI](https://www.wired.com/story/gauth-ai-math-homework-app/) 来快速获得答案。更注重课堂实践和讨论的课程计划可能有助于缓解这个问题。但仅仅关注家庭作业会忽略对学生们的另一个迫在眉睫的威胁。在未来几年，我预计青少年会更多地投入到漫长、发自内心的、有时不恰当的对话中，而不是与网上的陌生人，而是与 [Character.AI](https://character.ai/) 或 [Replika](https://replika.com/) 这样的甜言蜜语的聊天机器人中。

在人生的一个困难、尴尬的阶段，由于当代社交媒体的严苛关注，青少年可能会变得更加内向和反社会，依赖合成伴侣来理解周围的世界。早在 2024 年，佛罗里达州的一名青少年就是角色扮演聊天机器人的狂热用户，并在自杀前向 AI 倾诉了自残的想法， [据纽约时报报道](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/10/23/technology/characterai-lawsuit-teen-suicide.html)。教孩子们如何安全地使用 AI 不仅是为了避免 [虚假信息](https://www.wired.com/story/generative-ai-detection-gap/)，也是为了避免不真实的关系并保持与现实的联系。

生成式 AI 在 [课堂上](https://www.wired.com/story/what-aspects-of-teaching-should-remain-human/) 的优势是否超过了威胁，在 2025 年是一个有点有争议的问题。这些工具已经渗透到学生们的日常生活。作为教育工作者，为这些孩子提供知识和技能来驾驭周围的世界至关重要。毫无疑问地接受生成式 AI 可能并不明智，但盲目回避也可能同样具有灾难性。

为你服务，

瑞斯
